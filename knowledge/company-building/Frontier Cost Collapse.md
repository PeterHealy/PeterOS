# Frontier Cost Collapse

## Core Thesis

Frontier markets become real when a technology collapses the cost of access enough to turn episodic expedition into repeatable work. The access breakthrough does not just make known demand cheaper to serve; it can reveal demand that was previously invisible because customers assumed the work was impossible, scarce, or too expensive.

## Frontier Sequence

- Discovery proves that a domain is real, reachable, and valuable.
- Expedition explores the domain, maps it, and finds resources or use cases, but does so episodically and expensively.
- The access breakthrough collapses the cost of reaching or operating in the domain.
- Industrialization integrates the frontier into the broader economy and creates industries that could not exist at the old cost structure.

## Governing Metric

Every constrained frontier tends to have one cost metric that matters disproportionately.

- For space, the central metric was `$/kg to orbit`.
- For the ocean, Ulysses proposes `$/km2-month`: cost of active operation over one square kilometer of ocean for one month.
- For other frontiers, the useful question is: what unit of access or operation makes the rest of the market possible if it falls by 10x or 100x?

## Multiplicative Curves

Falling costs across different pillars are often multiplicative rather than additive.

- Better energy storage makes persistent hardware more viable.
- More persistent hardware creates more data.
- More data improves autonomy and AI.
- Better AI improves design, routing, fault detection, maintenance, and energy efficiency.
- Better operations increase utilization, which pushes effective cost down further.

This matters most when legacy industry architecture remains frozen around old assumptions. If multiple component curves improve while incumbents still design for scarcity, a new entrant can reorganize the stack around abundance.

## Physical AI Cost Collapse

[[sources/ai-agents/Travis Kalanick - Atoms Vision]] applies a similar frontier logic to land, mining, manufacturing, food, and transport. The claim is that physical abundance depends on reducing making and moving things toward the cost of minerals, energy, computation, and autonomous machine time.

In this frame, the frontier is not only a remote place like the ocean or space. It can be an ordinary physical workflow that has not yet been made software-addressable. The progress loop is:

1. Sense the current physical state.
2. Predict the near-future physical state.
3. Act through routing, dispatch, facilities, machines, or robots.
4. Use the resulting data and throughput to lower the effective cost of physical work.

The key company-building question is whether the team can turn a messy physical constraint into a repeatable control system before incumbents reorganize around the new cost curve.

## Latent Demand

Demand is shaped by perceived supply, not just real need.

- If buyers believe a service is scarce, slow, or expensive, they may not ask for it.
- A small existing market can hide a much larger latent market when supply is the constraint.
- Lower access cost can create new customer imagination: use cases appear once buyers believe the service can be bought repeatedly.

Subsea cable monitoring is a useful example from the ocean essay: if vessel supply constrains monitoring and repair, the current market size may understate the true need for persistent infrastructure.

## Conservation And Governance

Industrial capability is not automatically good stewardship. It can expand extraction. But in domains where abuse is already happening invisibly, monitoring and intervention infrastructure can also become the basis for better governance.

The key difference is whether the same cost collapse that enables commerce also enables measurement, enforcement, feedback loops, and accountability.

## Evaluation Questions

- What is the frontier's true governing cost metric?
- Which current market is obviously supply-constrained rather than demand-constrained?
- Which use cases are not being requested because customers assume they are impossible?
- Which component curves are improving independently, and do they multiply each other?
- Can a new entrant redesign the stack around the new cost structure?
- Does the access breakthrough create measurement and accountability, or only extraction?
- Can the company digitize a physical workflow enough to sense, predict, and control useful future states?
