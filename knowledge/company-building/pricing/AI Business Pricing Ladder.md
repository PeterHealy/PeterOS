# AI Business Pricing Ladder

## Core Thesis

AI products should not blindly copy mass-market AI pricing. A small AI business needs a price structure that can pay for acquisition, support, model usage, and founder time. For many narrow B2B or prosumer AI products, the better ladder is free sampling, a clear core plan, a high anchor tier, annual billing, and expansion revenue from happy customers.

## The Ladder

- Free tier: lets prospects sample the product, but should limit expensive models, heavy usage, and support.
- Core plan: the self-serve plan most buyers are expected to choose.
- Anchor plan: a much higher-priced tier that clarifies the product's serious-user ceiling and makes the core plan feel cheaper.
- Annual plan: pulls cash forward, funds acquisition, and increases buyer commitment.
- Setup or onboarding fee: fits narrow, painful business workflows where implementation, customization, or workflow design creates real value.
- Expansion path: lets happy customers move into higher usage, better limits, support, onboarding, or adjacent solved problems.

## Pricing Math

- Low-ticket monthly pricing needs volume. At $20/month, $20k MRR requires 1,000 paying customers.
- Higher-ticket niche pricing needs fewer buyers. At $300/month plus setup, the founder can focus on a smaller number of high-intent customers.
- Annual cash changes acquisition economics. A customer paying hundreds of dollars upfront can fund ads, onboarding, or founder-led sales before monthly subscriptions would have broken even.

## When Higher Pricing Is Plausible

- The buyer is a business or wealthy/professional user, not a casual consumer.
- The product solves a painful problem with obvious economic value.
- The niche is narrow enough that the product feels purpose-built.
- The product replaces labor, prevents loss, generates revenue, saves meaningful time, or handles an expensive workflow.
- The founder can explain why the AI product is not merely a generic wrapper around a broad model.

## Annual And Manual Support

Annual plans work better when the offer feels materially better than a discounted monthly plan. Early-stage founders can include onboarding, support, setup help, templates, or custom configuration because the goal is learning and revenue, not immediate infinite scalability.

The constraint is margin visibility. Manual extras are useful when they reveal onboarding friction, conversion blockers, and customer language. They become dangerous when every sale requires bespoke work that is not priced or tracked.

## Expansion Revenue

Existing satisfied customers are usually easier to monetize than strangers. Grandfather old pricing to avoid punishing early adopters, then create credible upgrade paths:

- more usage;
- better models;
- team seats or collaboration;
- support and onboarding;
- workflow-specific add-ons;
- adjacent problems in the same niche.

Expansion revenue can offset churn when retained customers increase spend faster than lost customers reduce revenue.

## AI Cost Posture

- Use tier limits before elaborate cost optimization.
- Reserve expensive models and long contexts for paid or higher-tier customers.
- Use cheaper models for background tasks when quality requirements are lower.
- Compact context and cache where it is simple.
- Do not let hypothetical million-user costs distract from the current bottleneck when the product has only a small number of customers.

## Caveats

- Higher prices do not fix weak positioning or weak demand.
- A fake or neglected anchor tier can erode trust.
- Annual billing creates stronger expectations around reliability, support, refunds, and product continuity.
- Setup fees need a clear reason; otherwise they look like friction.
- Outcome pricing, high subscriptions, and setup fees all require stronger proof of value than generic feature-led SaaS pricing.
