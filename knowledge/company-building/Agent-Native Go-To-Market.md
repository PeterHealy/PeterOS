# Agent-Native Go-To-Market

## Core Thesis

- In an agent-native business, the product surface is the agent while incumbent SaaS stays in the background as system-of-record infrastructure.
- The strongest entry point is usually a narrow workflow that is still held together by dashboards, spreadsheets, phone calls, and service labor.
- The business should aim to own the workflow end-to-end and charge against the business result when attribution is clear.

## Selection Criteria

- Start with one vertical workflow, not a general-purpose horizontal agent.
- Prefer repetitive, high-frequency work with clear pain and obvious handoffs.
- Prefer workflows where the agent can take actions, not just draft suggestions.
- Prefer categories where success can be measured in buyer-relevant terms such as renewals completed, revenue collected, tickets resolved, or hours removed from operations.
- Prefer markets where the founder or early team has direct operator knowledge of the workflow.

## Go-To-Market Pattern

- Sell the completed job, not the model or automation layer.
- Use existing systems of record as integration anchors so buyers do not need a rip-and-replace migration.
- Compress recurring implementation and services work into software wherever possible.
- Keep the initial promise narrow enough that the agent can actually deliver the result reliably.
- Pair product with a real distribution wedge such as niche audience, operator reputation, or media reach.

## Pricing Implications

- Outcome-based pricing fits best when the output is attributable and the buyer value is obvious.
- Hybrid pricing is safer when the outcome is lagged, shared across teams, or easy to dispute.
- Seat pricing is a poor fit when one agent can perform far more work than a human user.

## Operating Constraints

- Many categories still need approvals, audit trails, visibility, and exception handling.
- Not every incumbent is just a database; some also own trust, permissions, reporting, and procurement leverage.
- Services compression is easiest where the workflow is rules-heavy and low-exception.
- Distribution only becomes a moat if it converts into workflow trust and customer acquisition efficiency.

## Practical Screen

- Is the workflow ugly enough that users already patch it with spreadsheets, calls, or human service teams?
- Can the agent execute meaningful actions through APIs or other reliable system access?
- Is success measurable in a number the buyer already cares about?
- Is the exception rate low enough that human backstops stay small?
- Does the team know the workflow well enough to design around the real edge cases?
