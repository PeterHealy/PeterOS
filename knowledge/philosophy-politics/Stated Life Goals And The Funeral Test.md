# Stated Life Goals And The Funeral Test

## Core Idea

The funeral test asks: what would you want to be praised for at your funeral, and what would you want future historical mentions to say you contributed?

The more practical version is not morbid. It is a meaning and alignment practice: periodically write down what you are trying to achieve with your life, then let future versions of yourself compare your actual days against your stated goals.

## Why It Helps

- It turns "meaning" from a vague feeling into an inspectable claim.
- It makes daily choices legible as contributions toward, or distractions from, a larger aim.
- It separates your own goals from the goals other people may project onto you.
- It creates a record that later friends, family, collaborators, or historians could use to judge you by your own terms.
- It supports revision: if the stated goal no longer feels true, the mismatch is visible.

## The Practice

Write a short periodic essay answering:

- What am I trying to become useful for?
- What would I be proud to have my days accumulate into?
- What do I hope people can truthfully praise me for later?
- Which projects, habits, and relationships currently support that?
- Which parts of my calendar reveal a different goal than the one I claim?

The essay should be concrete enough that future-you can tell whether it was lived.

## Useful Distinction

There are at least two layers of evaluation:

- Other people's goals for you: status, money, career prestige, family expectations, institutional rewards.
- Your own stated goals: the contribution or way of living you explicitly chose.

Both can matter, but the second needs to be written down if it is going to survive mood, pressure, drift, and other people's narratives.
