# Human Provenance After AI Abundance

## Core Idea

If AI and robotics make material goods and services abundant, human value may shift toward provenance, status, trust, taste, intimacy, beauty, and shared experience. The scarce thing is not necessarily output quality. It is the knowledge that a particular human made, chose, performed, judged, or cared.

## Why This Matters

The strongest AI-displacement story says capital eventually substitutes for labor. If machines can do all work, labor loses bargaining power and capital owners capture nearly everything.

The human-provenance counterpoint is not that current jobs are safe. It is that human demand is not limited to material sufficiency. People compare, desire, admire, flirt, trust, imitate, collect, and gather around other people. Those drives can create new work even when machine output is cheaper or technically superior.

Packy McCormick's "costless sacrifice" variant adds a stricter condition: provenance becomes more valuable when the audience can perceive that the work cost the creator something. The scarcity is not just "a human touched this." It is that a human chose to pay a real cost in attention, taste, time, courage, or conviction.

## Mechanisms

- Human-made work can carry status because it is scarce, situated, and attributable.
- Costly human work can signal care because it required a choice; costless mass output cannot make the same claim.
- Imperfection can become part of value when it signals a real person rather than a synthetic process.
- Shared human artifacts create common ground in a way individualized AI outputs often do not.
- Beauty may become more investable if AI and robotics reduce the opportunity cost of human labor.
- Courtship, community, taste, and reputation can generate labor markets around being human, not merely around completing tasks.

## Heuristics

- Ask whether users care about provenance or only about function.
- Ask whether the work shows evidence of cost: research, lived contact, hard choices, taste, accountability, or personal risk.
- Expect AI to dominate utilitarian output first where cost, speed, and reliability matter most.
- Expect human premiums where identity, status, trust, intimacy, locality, or collective attention matter.
- Do not assume future jobs are legible from current job categories; look for new infrastructure that lets humans create or signal value in new ways.
- Treat "AI can make this better" and "humans will prefer this from humans" as separate questions.

## Tensions

- Provenance premiums may be uneven and status-driven, not a universal replacement for wages.
- Human-made value may cluster around creators, performers, leaders, craftspeople, communities, and trusted experts rather than everyone equally.
- AI-generated fakes make provenance infrastructure more important and harder.
- Material abundance does not by itself solve relative comparison, political power, or ownership of productive systems.
- Cost is an imperfect signal. Audiences can misread it, creators can perform it, and AI can imitate some external signs of care.
