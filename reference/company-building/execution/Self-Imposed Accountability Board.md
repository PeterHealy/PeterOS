# Self-Imposed Accountability Board

## What It Is

A lightweight personal operating system for exploratory or self-directed work. Every two weeks, send a small trusted group your sprint goals, a midpoint update, and an end-of-sprint review. The group does not need to manage you or respond. The accountability comes from making the commitment visible.

## When To Use It

- You are between structured roles and need momentum without prematurely committing to one path.
- You are exploring startup ideas, writing, advising, health routines, or other work where progress is easy to rationalize away.
- You want outside accountability but do not want a formal coach, manager, or advisory board.
- You need a repeatable rhythm that includes both work and personal goals.

## Basic Setup

1. Pick an initial board of 5-10 people whose opinion you respect.
2. Tell them there is no responsibility to reply.
3. Choose a two-week sprint cadence.
4. For each sprint, choose one theme.
5. Commit to a small number of goals: three professional goals and three personal goals.
6. Send a midpoint progress email.
7. Send an end-of-sprint summary with what shipped, what slipped, and what changed.
8. Start the next sprint with a new theme and a refreshed goal list.

## Sprint Email Template

```markdown
Subject: Sprint update: [theme]

Theme: [one phrase]

Work:
1. [goal with clear done condition]
2. [goal with clear done condition]
3. [goal with clear done condition]

Personal:
1. [goal with clear done condition]
2. [goal with clear done condition]
3. [goal with clear done condition]

Notes:
- What I am testing this sprint:
- What might get in the way:
- Optional ask:

Reminder: no reply needed. Sending this creates the accountability.
```

## Midpoint Update Template

```markdown
Subject: Mid-sprint update: [theme]

Green:
- [on-track goal]

Yellow:
- [at-risk goal and next action]

Red:
- [blocked or likely missed goal]

Adjustment:
- [what I am changing for the second half of the sprint]
```

## End-Of-Sprint Review Template

```markdown
Subject: Sprint review: [theme]

Done:
- [completed goals]

Missed:
- [missed goals and why]

Learned:
- [useful observation]

Next:
- [what the next sprint should test]
```

## Why It Works

- It creates a deadline without creating a boss.
- It turns vague exploration into a sequence of visible commitments.
- It keeps personal maintenance in the same system as professional output.
- It reduces the need for willpower because the next update is already implied.
- It gives the user a longitudinal record of what they repeatedly choose, avoid, or learn.

## Failure Modes

- Making the board too large before the habit is stable.
- Choosing goals that are too vague to review.
- Treating the board as an advice committee instead of an accountability surface.
- Letting guilt from missed goals replace diagnosis.
- Continuing the sprint cadence after the real goal has changed.

## Adaptations

- Use a private email list when the social signal matters.
- Use a shared doc when the historical record matters more than the audience.
- Use one trusted person if a board feels performative.
- Add a monthly retrospective if the same goals slip multiple sprints in a row.
- Keep a separate "ideas discovered while sprinting" section so exploration does not derail the committed sprint.
