# Dying As An AoE Spell

## Original Capture

this is more of a thought I had of something to write about or explore in my own time,

dying is one of the most impactful AoE spells in existence that we need to replicate when people are living. some line about tupac or something smelling the flowers when you're alive etc.

i felt a big repsonsibility when my colleague passed away to share this story and if I had said it when he was living it likely would have had good effect too. but I was only obliged to share it once he had passed. it wouldn't have occurred to me to think deeply about that action having a positive effect on me. if I had sent an email around to everyone without this horrible event having occurred would it have had the same impact? probably not. but now you would think these words stick with people as they think in their own quiet time about barry and the impact they had on them.

why is this the case? why can't we replicate this AoE when someone is alive?

## Core Question

Why do death, funerals, memorial emails, and public grief create a permission structure for praise, story-sharing, and moral attention that feels unnatural or less powerful while someone is alive?

## Exploration Angles

- Death creates urgency: people suddenly know there will be no later chance to say the thing.
- Death creates permission: sincere praise becomes socially legible instead of awkward, performative, or status-seeking.
- Death creates collective attention: many people are thinking about the same person at once, which turns private memory into shared ritual.
- Death lowers strategic ambiguity: the recipient cannot reward, reciprocate, reject, or be embarrassed by the praise.
- Death forces narrative compression: people search for the story that explains what someone meant.
- Living praise may need ritual design: the missing thing might not be the words, but the container that makes them feel proportionate.

## Possible Forms

- Essay: "Dying Is an AoE Spell"
- Short post: "Why do we wait until someone dies to say the thing everyone needed to hear?"
- Personal essay about Barry, the story shared after his death, and what would have changed if the same words had been said earlier.
- Practical ritual design: living eulogies, annual gratitude letters, founder/team appreciation memos, or "tell them while they can still hear it" dinners.
