## What This Source Is

A company vision page by Travis Kalanick announcing the renaming of City Storage Systems to Atoms and laying out a thesis for physical automation. The page frames Atoms as a portfolio of "atoms-based computers" across food, mining, and transport: physical systems that sense, predict, and control the state of the world.

## Source Summary

The page opens with Kalanick's personal return-to-building narrative after Uber and positions "Digitizing the Physical World" as his long-term work. He says City Storage Systems began as an "atoms-based computation" company focused on real estate and food, and that the renamed Atoms now expands into mining and transport.

The central thesis is that the next major progress era comes when growing, mining, manufacturing, and moving physical things become increasingly divorced from direct human labor. Software has automated language and math tasks; the remaining under-automated frontier is autonomy in the physical world. In Kalanick's framing, abundance arrives when making and moving physical goods is reduced toward minerals, energy, computation, and autonomous machines that can improve while humans sleep.

Land, materials, minerals, and energy are treated as first-order inputs, not background constraints. The page argues that physical AI companies need real estate development competence because they need places to mine, manufacture, store energy, and operate. This links the AI thesis to industrial supply, construction, and resource control.

The "Digitizing the Physical World" section gives the most reusable model. Physical automation should be approached like software: understand current state, predict future state, and control future state. Kalanick uses Uber as the example. Driver phones created a city-scale sensor network; if Uber could infer traffic lights, trash trucks, and vehicle movement, it could dispatch and route cars to save time. That makes the company a kind of "mini-time-machine" because it uses a digitized model of the city to change future physical outcomes.

Atoms' physical-AI stack is explicitly cross-disciplinary: sensors, compute, AI models, manipulation, software, operations research, manufacturing, real estate construction, energy, and chemistry. The page argues that no single company must own every layer, but cross-stack competence increases the chance of building useful industrial progress machines.

The robotics strategy is task-specific. Kalanick contrasts humanoids with "gainfully employed robots": machines best suited to a productive job. Humanoids may fit low-scale, diverse tasks in human-designed spaces, such as housework. But for high-throughput industrial tasks, like making 1,000 pancakes per hour, specialized machines can be faster, cheaper, denser, and more reliable than anthropomorphic robots.

The page closes by tying the company vision back to Kalanick's personal return. He frames Atoms not as a new direction but as a continuation of his work on physical-world computation.

## Key Ideas

- Physical AI is the automation of action in the world, not just cognition or language.
- A physical-world computer needs analogues to CPU, storage, and network: manufacturing, real estate, and transport.
- Industrial autonomy depends on materials, minerals, energy, land, and construction competence as much as models.
- The core physical automation loop is to understand current state, predict future state, and control future state.
- Dense sensor networks can make messy cities more software-addressable.
- The valuable output of digitization is not the model itself, but changed physical outcomes: faster pickup, better routing, lower cost, higher throughput.
- Form factor should follow the job. Humanoids fit diverse low-volume work; specialized machines fit high-volume industrial work.
- "Gainfully employed robots" are robots that can make a living by doing a productive job.

## Capture-Based Synthesis

Your capture points at a useful company-building distinction: physical AI is not just "robots plus AI." It is a full-stack operating model for sensing the current world, predicting near-future constraints, and acting through machines, routes, facilities, and land.

The Uber analogy is especially strong because it avoids science-fiction robotics. The "machine" is partly a distributed sensor network, partly a prediction system, partly a dispatch system, and partly a fleet of human-driven cars. That suggests a bridge from software-mediated operations to fully autonomous physical systems.

The humanoid section pairs well with [[knowledge/ai-agents/Robotics Data And Deployment Gradient]]. Both sources argue that near-term robotics value is likely to come from job-to-machine fit rather than human-shaped generality. Kalanick's version is more industrial and infrastructure-heavy; the Standard Bots source is more data-and-deployment-heavy.

## Claims Worth Tracking

- The cost per unit of intelligence is falling by 90% per year.
- Total capabilities and general intelligence increased nearly 1,000-fold over the prior three years.
- Physical-world autonomy requires physical AI computation and deep learning models that do not yet exist at the required efficiency.
- Atoms can build a cross-industry portfolio around food, mining, and transport as physical-world computation primitives.
- Specialized robots will outperform humanoids for many high-throughput industrial jobs.

## Caveats

- This is a founder/company vision page, so it mixes strategic argument, recruiting narrative, and brand positioning.
- The intelligence-cost and capability-scaling claims are not sourced on the page and should not be reused as hard facts without verification.
- The argument is strongest as a framework for industrial automation and less complete as an explanation of household robotics, labor impacts, or governance.
- The page uses abundance framing but does not deeply address safety, distributional effects, permitting, or political constraints.
