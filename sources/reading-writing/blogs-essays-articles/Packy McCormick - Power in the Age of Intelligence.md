## What This Source Is

A Not Boring strategy essay arguing that AI abundance changes the relevant question from "what software moats survive?" to "which companies own scarce, defensible positions that become more valuable when general-purpose inputs get cheaper?"

## Source Summary

McCormick opens by rejecting the narrow "AI versus SaaS moats" frame. The more important question is which companies benefit from newly abundant inputs such as code, intelligence, batteries, manufacturing learning curves, and distribution infrastructure.

The essay uses Stripe versus Adyen, Ramp versus Brex, SpaceX, the SaaS selloff, and the concentration of venture capital to argue for a "winner takes more" world. Technological change expands addressable markets and lets the best-positioned companies capture a larger share of industry value. The pattern is not new: railroads, telegraphs, electrification, containerization, aviation, and the internet all expanded market reach and pushed value toward top firms.

The strategic framework has three linked concepts. The `Schwerpunkt` is the industry constraint to attack. The `High Ground` is the scarce position won by breaking that constraint. Moats matter only after a company has captured something worth defending.

This reframes AI risk for incumbents and startups. Point-solution software businesses are vulnerable because abundant code makes marginal tooling easier to reproduce. Hardware alone is not a moat either, because other technical curves are also improving. The durable advantage is owning the scarce industry asset or bottleneck that lets the company turn abundant inputs into domination of an industry's economics.

McCormick also argues that innovation alone is insufficient. A company that breaks a constraint must also own or assemble the complementary assets needed to capture value: distribution, manufacturing, customer relationships, regulation, capital, supply, or other position-specific assets. Otherwise, another actor with those complements captures the profit.

The closing claim is an optimistic builder/investor thesis: the age of intelligence should make the most strategically positioned companies bigger, not smaller. The attractive problem is not defending small castles from AI, but identifying industries where a modern company can take the High Ground and expand outward.

## Key Ideas

- The central AI strategy question is who benefits from abundance, not only who is threatened by it.
- Company value is concentrating because technology increases the scale available to winners.
- Point-solution SaaS faces gravity when software production becomes cheap and generic.
- The scarce asset in an industry becomes more valuable when complementary inputs get cheaper.
- Startups should identify the binding industry constraint, concentrate force on it, and plan to own the complementary assets after breaking it.
- Moats are downstream of position; a moat around an unimportant position is not strategically useful.
- "Hardware is a moat" is too broad; some physical businesses will be crushed by better technical curves too.

## Capture-Based Synthesis

- The user's note captures the operational posture implied by the essay: prepare around a domain where many curves are about to break loose, then be ready to capture the upside when the bottleneck shifts.
- This is adjacent to [[knowledge/company-building/Frontier Cost Collapse]], but the emphasis here is strategic position after the cost collapse, not just the cost collapse itself.
- The most reusable phrase for company analysis is: "What is the scarce asset that gets more valuable as intelligence gets cheaper?"

## Claims Worth Tracking

- AI abundance will hurt point-solution software more than companies that own scarce industry positions.
- Venture capital concentration reflects a rational belief that the largest winners in a vintage may capture most of the value.
- Industry-specific bottleneck analysis is a better underwriting tool than generic SaaS metrics in the age of abundant software.
- Complementary assets may matter more than the original invention when imitation is easy.

## Caveats

- The essay leans toward venture-scale outcomes and may underweight durable smaller businesses with local, trust, or workflow-specific advantages.
- "Winner takes more" is plausible in many markets, but regulation, customer concentration, geography, and switching behavior can blunt it.
- The High Ground metaphor is useful, but can become vague unless translated into concrete industry constraints and owned assets.
