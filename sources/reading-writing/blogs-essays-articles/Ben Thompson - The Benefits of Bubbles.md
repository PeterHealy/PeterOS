## What This Source Is

A Stratechery article by Ben Thompson arguing that the AI bubble is probably real, but that some bubbles are productive because they coordinate people, capital, infrastructure, and invention around a shared vision of the future.

## Source Summary

Thompson opens by saying that unlike the recurring false alarms about tech bubbles in the 2010s, the AI moment looks like a genuine bubble. OpenAI and the hyperscalers are committing extraordinary sums to infrastructure relative to current AI revenue, and bubbles usually end with crashes, bankruptcies, and recessions. The point of the essay is that this may still be worth it if the bubble leaves behind durable capacity.

The first frame comes from Carlota Perez. In Thompson's reading, past technological revolutions often had an installation phase funded by speculative mania and followed by a deployment period after the bubble popped. The dotcom example is telecom fiber: much of the buildout was financially ruinous for the companies that borrowed to build it, but the physical capacity remained and became the low-cost substrate for the modern Internet.

The second frame comes from Byrne Hobart and Tobias Huber's concept of inflection bubbles. These are different from mean-reversion bubbles because they are based on a belief that the future will be categorically different, not merely that an existing asset should be repriced. Their main benefit is coordination. Once one part of the ecosystem invests in a future, adjacent investments become less risky: ISPs, search engines, e-commerce sites, ad-supported media, content, and consumer adoption can reinforce each other.

Thompson argues that the dotcom bubble created cognitive capacity as well as physical capacity. It brought the U.S. population online, trained a generation of web-native workers, pushed browser capabilities forward through competitive pressure, helped create the conditions for browser-based productivity software, and forced backend scaling innovations such as commodity x86 and Linux infrastructure. The bubble made many experiments happen at once.

The AI bubble is more ambiguous. GPUs are short-lived assets, so a bubble that only leaves behind depreciating chips would be disappointing from a Perez-style infrastructure perspective. Thompson is more optimistic about two longer-lived buildouts: fabs and power. AI has focused U.S. attention and capital on semiconductor manufacturing, and hyperscaler comments suggest that power is already a bottleneck. That makes power generation the most important potential residue of the bubble.

Power matters because it is a general-purpose input. If AI mania creates massive incentives to build power generation and pushes governments to reduce permitting friction, the payoff could extend far beyond AI. Thompson's strongest optimistic claim is that abundant low-marginal-cost power would make future inventions possible that currently never get tried because energy is not treated as negligible.

On cognitive capacity, Thompson is less worried. Many companies, researchers, and countries are working on AI; model leads are short-lived; consumer adoption is rapid; and funding is flowing to adjacent stack experiments such as new lithography, new chip architectures, and specialized AI hardware. A bubble creates permission and capital for many "might work" projects to run in parallel.

The essay closes by contrasting bubble risk with stagnation risk. AR and VR may have suffered from not having a broad startup bubble around them, remaining large-company budget lines rather than a mania that drew in many independent efforts. Thompson thinks the AI bubble has a quasi-spiritual motivational force. Some lab workers and founders believe they are building something world-historical, and even when that belief causes bad policy instincts, it can also supply the ambition and risk tolerance missing from stagnant eras.

## Key Ideas

- Some bubbles are productive because failed investments can leave behind cheap durable infrastructure.
- Perez's installation phase explains the physical residue of bubbles: fiber, railroads, factories, power generation, or other long-lived assets.
- Hobart and Huber's inflection-bubble frame explains the cognitive residue: many people build complementary parts of the same imagined future at the same time.
- The dotcom bubble's benefits were not just surviving internet companies; they included fiber, browser capabilities, web-native users, web workers, and scalable backend architectures.
- The AI bubble's weakest residue is GPUs because they depreciate quickly and are superseded by newer chips.
- The AI bubble's strongest potential residue is power generation, because abundant power is a broad input to future invention.
- Bubbles can restart risk appetite in stagnant periods by making speculative building feel urgent, legitimate, and socially contagious.
- The relevant comparison is not bubble versus no downside. It is bubble plus crash versus stagnation plus risk aversion.

## Your Captures

Submitted highlight email:

> The fundamental utility of inflection bubbles comes from their role as coordinating mechanisms. When one group makes investments predicated on a particular vision of the future, it reduces the risk for others seeking to build parts of that vision. For instance, the existence of internet service providers and search engines made e-commerce sites a better idea; e-commerce sites then encouraged more ad-dependent business models that could profit from directing consumers. Ad-dependent businesses then created more free content, which gave the ISPs a better product to sell. Each sector grew as part of a virtuous circle.

> Perez didn’t deny the pain: in fact, she noted that similar crashes marked previous revolutions, including the Industrial Revolution, railways, electricity, and the automobile. In each case the bubbles were not regrettable, but necessary: the speculative mania enabled what Perez called the “Installation Phase”, where necessary but not necessarily financially wise investments laid the groundwork for the “Deployment Period”. What marked the shift to the deployment period was the popping of the bubble; what enabled the deployment period were the money-losing investments.

> At the same time, this is also encouraging: the fastest way to restart growth — and hopefully at an even higher rate than the fifty years that preceded this stagnation — is to have massive economic incentives to build, combined with massive government incentives to eliminate red tape. AI provides both, and my hope is that the fact we are already hitting the power wall means that growth gets started that much sooner.
>
> It’s hard to think of a more useful and productive example of a Perez-style infrastructure buildout than power. It’s sobering to think about how many things have never been invented because power has never been considered a negligible input from a cost perspective; if AI does nothing more than spur the creation of massive amounts of new power generation it will have done tremendous good for humanity. Indeed, if you really want to push on the bubble benefit point, wiping away the cost of building new power via bankruptcy of speculative investors — particularly if a lot of that power has low marginal fuel costs, like solar or nuclear — could be transformative in terms of what might be invented in the future.

## Capture-Based Synthesis

- Your selected passages isolate the essay's useful model: the good version of a bubble is not that investors make money, but that speculative belief coordinates complementary bets.
- The Perez passage makes the finance/infrastructure distinction: bad financial returns can still create good real-world capacity if the failed assets remain useful.
- The power passage is the strongest AI-era implication. If AI forces a buildout of generation, transmission, and permitting capacity, the long-term benefit may not be "AI companies win"; it may be "energy stops being a limiting input for many future industries."
- This connects directly to [[knowledge/company-building/Frontier Cost Collapse]]: cheap power can be a governing input that unlocks latent demand and makes previously uneconomic markets visible.

## Claims Worth Tracking

- Whether AI capital expenditure keeps shifting from GPUs toward durable assets such as power, datacenters, fabs, grid upgrades, and new chip-manufacturing techniques.
- Whether power bottlenecks actually produce faster permitting, new generation, and transmission buildout, or mostly higher prices and political backlash.
- Whether AI-adjacent experimentation produces durable stack breakthroughs outside frontier model labs.
- Whether the eventual AI crash, if it comes, leaves cheap reusable assets or mostly stranded specialized capacity.
- Whether bubble psychology improves risk tolerance for founders and researchers, or encourages too much capital to chase shallow wrappers.

## Caveats

- The article is intentionally pro-bubble relative to conventional financial caution; it gives less attention to distributional damage, fraud, labor shock, and geopolitical risk.
- The dotcom analogy may overstate how reusable AI infrastructure will be if the most expensive assets are short-lived chips and specialized datacenters.
- Power buildout is not automatically benign. It depends on generation mix, grid design, land use, permitting quality, and whether cheap power is broadly accessible.
- Stagnation is a real risk, but not every bubble is an inflection bubble. Some manias destroy capital without leaving much durable capacity.
