## What This Source Is

A Will Manidis essay about language, dictionaries, English, scripture, AI, and the order of attention before building. It contrasts James Murray's Oxford English Dictionary posture of witness with Noah Webster's American posture of engineering.

## Source Summary

The essay begins with James Murray's OED scriptorium: a physical system for receiving, sorting, and interpreting millions of quotation slips. Murray's dictionary did not ask only what words should mean; it traced what they had meant across history through actual use.

Manidis contrasts Murray with Noah Webster. Murray is the naturalist, observing a living language with patience. Webster is the engineer, intentionally reshaping spelling and usage to build a distinct American language. Both postures are valuable, but they imply different theological and civilizational assumptions.

The essay then connects Murray's naturalist posture to English intellectual life, gardens, Orwell's account of English privateness, the King James Bible, Tyndale, and the formation of English as a world language. Manidis argues that English carries the cadence and moral architecture of scripture more deeply than modern speakers notice.

The AI turn is that large language models are built from English and now generate vast quantities of language without human lives directly attached to the words. Murray's question for a word was: who said this, when, and what did they mean? Increasingly, generated text can have no satisfying answer.

Manidis does not treat this as pure despair. He argues that the vessel of English still carries deep inheritance even when models do not know what they carry. The task is to recognize and attend to that inheritance.

The closing Genesis frame gives the essay its rule: naming comes before tending. Before engineering the garden, Adam names the beasts. Before building with language machines, builders should attend to the language, history, and created order they are handling.

## Key Ideas

- Murray's OED was an act of witness to living language, not just an act of definition.
- Webster represents a more American engineering posture toward language.
- The order matters: observe, name, and attend before building and reshaping.
- English carries historical and scriptural inheritances that modern speakers often use unknowingly.
- LLM-generated language can lack a human life, intention, or occasion behind it.
- The right response to language machines is not panic but deeper attention to what language carries.

## Claims Worth Tracking

- AI-generated language will make provenance and intention harder to recover.
- Language models inherit cultural and religious structures embedded in training language even when they do not understand them.
- Builders who attend before engineering may build better with AI than those who treat language as infinitely malleable material.

## Caveats

- The essay makes broad civilizational and theological claims from literary-historical material.
- It may overstate the unity or sacred continuity of English.
- The critique of generated language should be balanced against the fact that humans also produce low-intention or instrumental language.
