## What This Source Is

Casey Handmer's short blog post revisits the Fermi paradox and argues that one underweighted explanation is observational: if expansionist aliens travel at a high fraction of light speed, the light announcing their approach arrives only shortly before they do. That can make the intermediate historical phase between "we detect aliens" and "aliens arrive" vanishingly brief.

## Source Summary

Handmer frames the Fermi paradox in the usual way: the universe is old, the galaxy is small on light-speed timescales, life may be common, intelligence may be an evolutionary strategy, and technological life may produce detectable signatures. If all of those are true, the absence of visible aliens needs explanation.

He reviews several familiar candidates. Humanity has barely searched: telescopes are small relative to the scale of the galaxy, the exoplanet census is young, atmospheric spectra are scarce, and SETI has covered only a tiny slice of the search space. Interstellar travel could also be impossible or effectively impractical, because current probes would take enormous times to reach nearby stars and relativistic travel introduces severe engineering hazards. A Great Filter could sit in our past or future, but Handmer notes that a single civilization overcoming the filters could still fill the galaxy quickly.

His preferred explanation is related to Robin Hanson's Grabby Aliens model but emphasizes the light-cone geometry of observation. If alien civilizations expand near light speed, observers do not see the expansion in real time. They see only the past light cone, and the light from a launch or frontier is only slightly ahead of the actual travelers. For a civilization traveling at 99 percent of light speed from 1000 light years away, the warning time would be about 10 years; at 99.9 percent, about one year.

That changes the intuitive picture of visible galactic colonization. At low expansion speeds, an alien domain might appear as a growing sphere in the night sky before it reaches us. At relativistic speeds, the apparent visible region narrows because light from off-axis arrivals has not yet reached us. In the limiting case, even perfect observation may give little warning before contact.

Handmer therefore sees two plausible extremes: interstellar travel is basically impossible, or relativistic interstellar travel is easier than expected and expansion fronts are hard to observe before arrival. He favors the second, but concedes it still leaves a deep anthropic puzzle: if such travel is possible, no traveling alien civilization can have swept through the galaxy in the billions of years before humans appeared.

## Key Ideas

- The Fermi paradox is not only about whether aliens exist; it is also about what observation should look like under different travel speeds.
- Current human searches are extremely weak relative to the scale and opacity of the galaxy.
- The "not here yet, but detected" phase may be extremely short if incoming expansion is near light speed.
- Grabby alien expansion fronts look less like large visible spheres and more like narrow, delayed past-light-cone signatures as speed approaches light speed.
- Relativistic travel can reduce both warning time for observers and experienced travel time for travelers.
- The hard cases are the extremes: interstellar travel may be impossible, or relativistic travel may be possible enough that expansion is almost impossible to see in advance.

## Capture-Based Synthesis

This source is useful as a correction to one common Fermi-paradox intuition. The absence of obvious expanding alien domains does not only update beliefs about life rarity or filters; it also depends on the observability of expansion. At high expansion speeds, the sky can remain apparently empty until shortly before contact.

That does not dissolve the Great Filter. It moves some explanatory weight from "why do we not see them?" to "why did no relativistic expansion wave begin in our galaxy early enough to have already reached us?" Handmer's account is therefore compatible with [[knowledge/philosophy-politics/The Great Filter]], but it weakens naive claims that empty skies straightforwardly rule out fast grabby civilizations outside our recent light-cone warning window.

## Claims Worth Tracking

- If alien expansion happens at 99 percent of light speed from 1000 light years away, the maximum visual warning is on the order of 10 years.
- If alien expansion happens at 99.9 percent of light speed from 1000 light years away, the warning window drops to roughly one year.
- Near-light-speed expansion makes pre-contact detection much harder than slower Grabby Aliens diagrams suggest.
- Vera Rubin-class sky surveys might detect incoming relativistic spacecraft only within a short warning horizon, if at all.
- The argument still requires an anthropic explanation for why no earlier galactic expansion wave has already arrived.

## Caveats

- The piece is explicitly a quick note, not a full quantitative paper.
- The observability argument depends on assumptions about alien travel speeds, propulsion signatures, detectability, and expansion behavior.
- It does not settle whether interstellar travel is feasible, only how the Fermi paradox looks if high-speed travel is feasible.
- It should be read alongside Hanson's Great Filter and Grabby Aliens work rather than as a replacement for them.
