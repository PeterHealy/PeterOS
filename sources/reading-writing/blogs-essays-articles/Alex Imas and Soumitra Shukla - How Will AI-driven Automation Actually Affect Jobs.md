# Alex Imas and Soumitra Shukla - How Will AI-driven Automation Actually Affect Jobs?

## What This Source Is

A Ghosts of Electricity essay by Alex Imas and Soumitra Shukla arguing that AI "exposure" is not the same as displacement risk. The piece uses the O-ring model of complementary tasks to explain why partial automation can raise worker productivity and wages, while low-dimensional jobs with inelastic demand can still face severe displacement.

## Source Summary

The article starts from the public confusion around AI exposure scores. It cites the "GPTs are GPTs" paper and Andrej Karpathy's viral exposure dashboard as examples of a useful measurement idea that gets misread as a direct forecast of job destruction. The authors stress that exposure means AI can affect or speed up tasks; it does not by itself say whether a worker is more valuable, less valuable, hired, or fired.

Their central correction is that jobs are bundles of tasks, and many real jobs are not additive task lists. In a standard exposure index, automating task A simply adds to the impact score and leaves task B unchanged. In many jobs, though, tasks are complements: the output only works if many steps are done well together. A cook who misses one crucial recipe step, a drummer who is almost on beat, or a programmer whose code almost works can ruin the final product.

The authors connect this to Michael Kremer's O-ring theory, where production is multiplicative rather than linear. Small quality gains compound across tasks, and one weak component can collapse the value of the whole output. In the AI setting, this means partial automation can help workers by removing some tasks from their time budget. If the automated tasks are good enough, the worker can focus more attention on the remaining human tasks, and those improvements multiply through the job's output.

This is the article's "focus effect." Automation can raise output even when the automated task is slightly worse than the worker's old manual performance, because the worker becomes much better at the remaining tasks. In these cases AI complements labor rather than substituting for it, so wages can rise. The authors use medicine, radiology, and research assistants as examples where AI handles routine components while humans focus on judgment, complex cases, communication, or ideas.

Higher productivity still does not guarantee more jobs. The employment effect depends on demand elasticity. If AI makes workers far more productive in a sector where lower prices do not create much extra demand, firms can meet demand with fewer workers. If demand is elastic, lower prices can expand output enough that firms hire more workers even though each worker is more productive. The piece frames this as a labor-market version of Jevons' paradox.

The second key variable is job dimensionality: how many core tasks must be automated before the job itself can disappear. Low-dimensional jobs are more vulnerable because automating one or two core tasks can eliminate the whole role, and because firms have a stronger incentive to invest when automation removes an entire wage bill rather than merely improving one task in a larger human workflow.

This flips some common AI-risk intuitions. Management consultants may look highly exposed because many of their digital tasks can be augmented, but that exposure may increase their value if client judgment, relationship management, synthesis, and implementation remain human bottlenecks. Truck drivers may look less exposed on a task index, but if autonomous driving solves the core driving task on constrained routes, the job is much closer to full displacement. Warehousing is treated similarly: narrow task bundles plus machine-designed operating environments make automation incentives stronger.

The final takeaway is that the relevant object is not average task exposure. It is the structure of bottlenecks: whether tasks are complements, whether demand can absorb productivity gains, how many core tasks the job has, and whether firms can justify the adoption cost by replacing the job rather than merely improving one activity.

## Key Ideas

- AI exposure measures are useful maps of model reach, but they are weak displacement forecasts.
- Many jobs are O-ring systems where tasks are complements, not separable additive units.
- Partial automation can raise human productivity through the focus effect: fewer remaining tasks get more attention, and quality gains compound.
- Employment effects depend on whether demand expands enough to absorb higher productivity.
- Low-dimensional jobs are more exposed to full displacement because automating a small number of core tasks can remove the role.
- Firm incentives matter: adoption is costly, so companies invest harder when automation can replace a job rather than merely improve one task.
- Trucking and warehousing may be more exposed to displacement than many white-collar jobs that score higher on digital AI exposure indices.
- The risk model should combine task complementarity, demand elasticity, job dimensionality, and adoption incentives.

## Capture-Based Synthesis

- The source is useful because it gives a sharper vocabulary for AI labor risk than "AI can do X percent of tasks."
- "Exposure" can be good news when AI removes routine work from a high-dimensional complementary job and lets the human spend more time on judgment, coordination, taste, or trust.
- The dangerous case is not necessarily the most digitally exposed job. It is the job where one automatable bottleneck is close to the whole role and the firm can capture the savings from redesigning around machines.
- This complements [[sources/reading-writing/blogs-essays-articles/The Kobeissi Letter - What If AI Doesnt Actually End The World]]: demand expansion can turn productivity gains into more employment, but only when demand is elastic enough.
- It also complicates broad AI-acceleration arguments in [[sources/reading-writing/blogs-essays-articles/Robin Hanson - AI is Acceleration]] by separating capability exposure from organizational adoption incentives.

## Claims Worth Tracking

- Exposure dashboards will keep being misread unless they explicitly separate task augmentation from job displacement.
- Low-dimensional physical jobs such as long-haul trucking and warehouse picking may face more abrupt displacement risk than their exposure scores imply.
- High-dimensional knowledge jobs may see wage gains if AI mostly clears routine tasks and leaves trust, judgment, and coordination as bottlenecks.
- Autonomous trucking adoption may become a better labor-displacement signal than LLM task exposure rankings.
- AI adoption in medicine and research may initially increase demand for skilled humans if better output expands the addressable work.

## Caveats

- The O-ring model is stylized; real jobs mix complementary, substitutable, and socially negotiated tasks.
- Demand elasticity is hard to estimate for many AI-affected services because lower prices may create new categories rather than simply increasing volume.
- The trucking and warehousing examples depend on the reliability, regulation, liability, and route constraints of physical automation, not only model capability.
- A high-dimensional job can become low-dimensional over time if many supporting tasks are automated elsewhere before the final bottleneck is solved.
