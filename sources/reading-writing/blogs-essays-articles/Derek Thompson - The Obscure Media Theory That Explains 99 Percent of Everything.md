## What This Source Is

A Derek Thompson interview with Joe Weisenthal about "the orality theory of everything." The source uses Walter Ong, Marshall McLuhan, and modern media shifts to argue that digital communication is moving society away from literate, solitary, abstract thinking and back toward oral, conversational, viral, social modes.

## Source Summary

Thompson introduces the theory as a way to think through broad second-order effects of media change. The core distinction is between orality and literacy. Oral cultures rely on speech, memory, repetition, rhythm, social learning, and flexible cultural memory. Literate cultures fix words in place, enabling solitary reading, abstraction, formal logic, and complex institutions.

Weisenthal argues that the internet is making communication more oral again, not only because people speak more through audio and video, but because digital text increasingly behaves like conversation: back-and-forth, performative, memorable, viral, and optimized for social response.

The interview emphasizes that literacy changed consciousness. Written language let people step back from immediate social interaction, contemplate ideas privately, and build abstract systems. Modern digital media pulls people back into immersive, social, always-on environments that resemble the ear more than the eye.

The "digital orality" section is especially important. Tweets, TikToks, podcasts, radio, and video can be recorded, so they are not purely oral. But the archive is becoming less stable and less trusted. AI-generated media, disappearing content, and malleable records may make history feel more like oral culture again, where narratives shift with current values.

The conversation applies the theory to politics, attention, reading decline, short-form video, and AI. Trump's nicknames, viral epithets, and performative media are treated as nearly Homeric oral devices. Short-form video becomes an attractor state for media because it is social, memorable, embodied, and low-friction.

The value of the source is not that orality explains literally everything. It is that the oral/literate distinction gives a strong lens for why politics, media, memory, expertise, and attention feel different in a post-literate digital environment.

## Key Ideas

- Literacy enabled solitary abstraction, fixed records, formal reasoning, and many modern institutions.
- Digital media can make text behave like speech: conversational, performative, repetitive, and socially optimized.
- Oral communication prioritizes memorability, rhythm, repetition, and virality.
- The internet feels immersive like hearing: hard to step outside and hard to close off.
- AI and synthetic media weaken trust in the archive and may make records feel malleable again.
- Short-form video may be the attractor state of post-literate media.
- Politics benefits from oral devices such as epithets, slogans, and repeated narrative packets.
