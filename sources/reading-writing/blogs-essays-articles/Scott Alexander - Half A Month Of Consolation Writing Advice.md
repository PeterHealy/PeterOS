## What This Source Is

Scott Alexander's article is a set of short writing lessons for Inkhaven participants, framed as compensation for missing half of a blogging bootcamp. It is mainly about nonfiction blogging: how to make prose honest, direct, original, topic-constrained, and worth the reader's limited attention.

## Source Summary

The article's central principle is that bad writing often begins as small dishonesty. A writer tries to smuggle in the real point beneath a more presentable topic, overstates a recovery arc, writes an explainer without a real question, or hedges assertions until the sentence is no longer doing clean work. Alexander argues that English punishes this microdishonesty by making prose feel awkward and unclear.

The style advice is framed as temporary discipline rather than permanent law. Beginners should practice avoiding passive voice, adverbs, excessive intensifiers, hedging, first-person tics, "obviously", tangled sentence structures, and stale cliches. The point is not that great writers never use these tools, but that constraint trains the ear. After the mountaintop discipline, the writer can break rules more deliberately.

The article also argues that nonfiction needs direct contact with the world. Blog posts that merely recycle blogosphere opinions are weak because they add another layer of telephone between the reader and reality. Useful posts inject primary experience, original research, close reading of primary sources, or at least a serious synthesis that has not already been done well.

Alexander distinguishes between brilliant prose and competent usefulness. A few writers can make almost any topic compelling; most writers should choose topics where they can provide concrete value to a specific audience. Even over-discussed topics can still be open if nobody has done the hard work of giving a detailed, honest, canonical treatment.

The later sections focus on reader attention and nonfiction engines. The writer has a short "runway" created by the title, recommendation, or initial curiosity; it should not be wasted on throat-clearing or definitions the reader already knows. Conflict and mystery help nonfiction hold attention because they give the reader a reason to continue. The final test is whether the reader could trivially regenerate the piece from a prompt. If yes, the writer has not added enough.

## Key Ideas

- Small dishonesty makes prose worse before readers can consciously name the problem.
- Style rules are best treated as training constraints: practice the discipline until the underlying judgment improves.
- Avoiding adverbs, hedging, first-person tics, "obviously", passive constructions, and tangled sentences forces clearer assertion.
- Explainers are weak when they are written from obligation rather than from a real question or thesis.
- The five-paragraph essay is not good style, but it teaches thesis, support, topic sentences, and closure.
- A writer contributes to the blogosphere by bringing in primary experience, primary sources, or serious effort, not by recycling already-digested takes.
- Ideas are everywhere if the writer is willing to do the work of finding the under-answered version of an over-discussed topic.
- Nonfiction benefits from conflict, mystery, and a clear reason the reader cannot just regenerate the essay from common sense.

## Claims Worth Tracking

- Writing blocks often come from trying not to say the exact thing one believes.
- Constraints that look simplistic at the sentence level can train useful judgment at the essay level.
- The main scarcity in online writing is not topics, but serious original effort applied to topics.
- Overused subjects can still produce strong essays when the author supplies neglected detail, primary evidence, or an unusually honest framing.
