## What This Source Is

A Not Boring essay by Packy McCormick about AI-generated abundance, the collapse of effort as a signal, and the enduring value of work that visibly costs the creator something. The essay uses King David's refusal to offer a costless sacrifice as its governing metaphor.

## Source Summary

McCormick opens with the story from 2 Samuel in which King David refuses Araunah's offer of free land, oxen, and wood for an altar. David insists on paying because an offering that costs nothing is not a real sacrifice. McCormick turns this into the essay's central claim: humans demand cost as proof of care, sincerity, and value.

The essay applies that idea first to the labor market. Drawing on Matt Darling's argument about "Tinder-ization" of hiring, McCormick says LLMs have made mass job applications cheaper and cover letters less informative. When everyone can cheaply customize applications, applicants still have to do it to keep up, but the signal collapses. The person who deeply wants one specific role may become harder to distinguish from hundreds of costless applications.

McCormick then connects this to writing. AI lets people publish competent essays quickly, creating many "pretty good" versions of ideas before a slower writer has finished wrestling with them. The danger is not only that machine-assisted versions are lower quality; it is that the existence of adequate versions can prevent more demanding, idiosyncratic, deeply worked versions from being made. In his phrase, "The pretty good is the enemy of the potentially great."

The same pattern appears in code and other token-producing work. McCormick is skeptical that graphs of AI-authored commits or AI-written words necessarily imply a productivity singularity. They may instead show token inflation: more visible activity, not necessarily more real output. He summarizes his AI trade as "short ASI, long tokens."

To make the broader historical point, McCormick invokes James Beniger's *The Control Revolution*. Industrialization created a crisis of control: material production outran the old information systems, forcing innovations in bureaucracy, telecommunications, logistics, schedules, computing, and mass media. McCormick argues that AI creates the inverse problem. Information can now be produced faster than the material reality it is supposed to represent. Society has gone off the gold standard for information.

The essay's practical conclusion is personal rather than systemic. McCormick does not know how to stop the downward spiral of more applications, more words, more commits, and more pseudo-productivity. But individuals can refuse the race by choosing to pay a real cost: research longer, edit harder, make something only they can make, and let the cost of care show through. That sacrifice may or may not be rewarded externally, but he argues it is rewarded internally and perhaps spiritually.

## Key Ideas

- Costless sacrifice is not sacrifice; effort matters because it signals care, choice, and sincerity.
- AI collapses many effort signals by making customized applications, essays, and code cheaper to produce.
- The Red Queen race appears when a cheap new capability gives no durable advantage but becomes mandatory because everyone else can use it.
- The problem with AI abundance is not only low-quality output; it is the crowding out of work that would have been better because it required deep wrestling.
- Token volume is not the same as productivity, meaning, taste, or economic output.
- AI may create an inverse Control Revolution: information production outruns the material or experiential reality that information is supposed to describe.
- Human-made work may retain value when the audience can feel that the creator paid a real cost.

## Capture-Based Synthesis

- The passage you selected is the essay's answer to AI-era token inflation: opt out by making the work actually cost something.
- "Pay the shekels" is a compact rule for creative practice: choose the higher-friction version when the friction is what lets the work carry soul, taste, and conviction.
- The theological framing matters because it changes the reward function. The sacrifice is not only a market signal to readers; it is also part of what gives the creator meaning.
- This source strengthens the existing wiki thread that human provenance is valuable after AI abundance, but it adds a sharper criterion: provenance is most legible when the work bears evidence of cost.

## Claims Worth Tracking

- AI-generated cover letters reduce the value of cover-letter customization as a hiring signal.
- The application-to-recruiter ratio has grown sharply enough to make human review unreliable for many roles.
- AI-authored GitHub commits and AI-written words may grow faster than measured productivity.
- Audiences will continue to reward visibly costly human work even when machine output is technically competent.
- The "information gold standard" metaphor may become a useful way to separate real-world work from token inflation.

## Caveats

- The essay is strongest as cultural interpretation, not as a quantified labor-market model.
- The "cost" of work is not automatically virtuous; effort can be wasted, performative, or misallocated.
- Audiences cannot always detect real cost, especially when AI can imitate signs of care.
- The practical answer is individual and craft-oriented; it does not solve the systemic coordination problem of mass costless output.
