# Packy McCormick and Will O'Brien - The Great Blue Frontier

## What This Source Is

A long Not Boring essay co-written by Packy McCormick and Will O'Brien, co-founder and President of Ulysses. The essay argues that the ocean is Earth's last major under-industrialized frontier, and that a convergence of autonomy, energy, communications, and AI can collapse the cost of persistent ocean work.

## Source Summary

The essay opens by contrasting the remembered Space Race with the largely forgotten ocean frontier of the 1960s. Kennedy, Cousteau, SEALAB, Conshelf, and dozens of underwater habitats once made the ocean feel like a parallel national project to space. That ambition collapsed after Berry Cannon's 1969 death during SEALAB 3, the Vietnam-era shift in priorities, and a cultural turn from ocean exploration toward ocean protection.

The authors argue that this retreat did not produce an untouched ocean. It produced a poorly monitored, pre-industrial domain where illegal fishing, labor abuse, overfishing, species loss, cable vulnerability, and ecological interventions happen with weak visibility and weak enforcement. Their recurring claim is that conservation without monitoring is fragile: line-drawing on maps does not protect a system that cannot be seen, measured, or acted on.

The ocean is framed as strategically and economically central despite being obscure. It covers most of the planet, carries most internet and trade traffic, contains massive biological and mineral resources, and still remains poorly mapped and poorly instrumented. The authors emphasize the unread biological library: most ocean species are unknown to science, while marine organisms have already produced important drugs and research tools such as Ziconotide, Trabectedin, and GFP.

The economic model is the frontier sequence: discovery proves the frontier exists, expedition explores it episodically, an access breakthrough collapses the cost of reaching and working there, and industrialization integrates the frontier into the broader economy. In space, the key metric was cost per kilogram to orbit. For the ocean, Ulysses proposes a governing metric: cost of active operation over one square kilometer for one month, or `$/km2-month`.

The article explains why the ocean did not get a SpaceX-like cost collapse earlier. Surface conditions are harsh, saltwater is corrosive, biofouling degrades equipment, power is difficult, communication through water is limited, and pressure rises with every ten meters of depth. Unlike space vacuum, underwater engineering conditions change continuously with depth. This pushed ocean work toward expensive crewed vessels, campaign-based expeditions, and scarce high-cost autonomous vehicles.

The authors argue that a "Curve Convergence" is now underway. Between roughly 2020 and 2025, multiple technology curves crossed useful thresholds: renewable and battery systems, autonomy and edge AI, communications and sensing, and lower-cost manufacturing. Their claim is not just that each curve helps independently, but that together they make a new architecture possible: persistent, autonomous, vertically integrated ocean infrastructure.

The market map includes surface and subsea platforms, sensing networks, autonomous ships, electric hydrofoils, ocean data systems, deep-sea mining companies, humane seafood automation, and Ulysses' own integrated stack. The pitch for Ulysses is explicitly SpaceX-like: vertically integrate the whole operational stack and price for scale rather than scarcity.

The conservation case is not passive preservation. The Russ George iron-fertilization story is used to argue that active ecological intervention failed politically and scientifically because the ocean lacked persistent monitoring. With real-time sensors and autonomous vehicles, interventions such as reef restoration, invasive species removal, pollution response, rewilding, and carbon experiments could be measured and adjusted rather than attempted blind.

The final thesis is that commerce and conservation can become symbiotic when the same infrastructure serves both. Cable monitoring, offshore energy, fisheries, insurance, defense, and science can all generate data that improves ocean understanding. The article is optimistic, but it also depends heavily on the claim that industrialization will increase monitoring, accountability, and restoration rather than merely expanding extraction.

## Key Ideas

- The ocean was once imagined as a frontier comparable to space, but that cultural and institutional ambition collapsed after the 1960s.
- A pre-industrial ocean is not an untouched ocean; it is an under-monitored domain where abuse and extraction can remain invisible.
- Unknown marine biology is a massive latent knowledge asset, with prior examples already producing drugs and research tools.
- Frontier industrialization follows a sequence: discovery, expedition, access-cost breakthrough, then broad economic integration.
- The ocean's missing railroad is a persistent low-cost infrastructure layer for sensing, communication, transport, autonomy, and intervention.
- The relevant cost metric is not just vehicle cost but the cost of active ocean work over area and time.
- Multiple falling-cost curves can multiply each other when a legacy industry architecture has remained frozen around high-cost assumptions.
- Conservation may require industrial capability: persistent visibility, measurement, enforcement, and feedback loops.

## Capture-Based Synthesis

- Falling costs across pillars are often multiplicative, not additive.
- Market demand is partly shaped by perceived supply; if customers assume ocean monitoring or intervention is scarce and expensive, they may not express demand for use cases that would become obvious under a lower-cost supply curve.
- The supply-constrained subsea cable monitoring market is a useful analogy for other categories where apparent market size may understate latent demand.
- The essay's environmental argument is most interesting when it treats industrial capability as the prerequisite for feedback loops, not as a guarantee of good stewardship.

## Claims Worth Tracking

- The global AUV market is still extremely small and expensive relative to the scale of the ocean.
- Subsea cable monitoring and repair may be more constrained by available vessels and tools than by end demand.
- Seagrass restoration is a high-leverage ocean-climate intervention if the quoted carbon and fishery support numbers hold up.
- Russ George's 2012 iron-fertilization experiment remains a cautionary example of intervention without measurement.
- The "ocean railroad" may become investable if `$/km2-month` drops enough to reveal latent demand.

## Caveats

- The essay is partly a company thesis for Ulysses, whose co-founder co-wrote the piece.
- Some environmental and market claims should be independently checked before being used as investment or policy premises.
- The core optimism depends on governance: lower-cost access could improve stewardship, but it could also accelerate extraction if incentives and oversight are poor.
- The article's "industrialization protects the frontier" analogy is directionally useful but historically incomplete; many frontiers were industrialized through exploitation before protection improved.
