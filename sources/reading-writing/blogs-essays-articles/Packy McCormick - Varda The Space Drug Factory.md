## What This Source Is

A Not Boring company essay about Varda Space Industries, co-written with biotech writer Elliot Hershberg. The piece argues that cheaper launch, SpaceX-trained talent, pharma economics, and dual-use reentry demand make commercial space manufacturing commercially plausible now rather than merely science fiction.

## Source Summary

McCormick frames Varda as an intersection company: part space company, part biotech company, and part hypersonic testing provider. The immediate occasion is Varda's first launch, a commercial space factory designed to orbit, test drug-manufacturing capabilities, and return a payload to Earth.

The article begins with the macro shift that made Varda possible. SpaceX did not only reduce launch cost; it also made rideshare launches more flexible and trained a cohort of engineers who know how to move quickly on hard aerospace problems. Once getting to orbit became less exotic, the obvious next strategic question became what valuable work could happen there.

The business problem is that space manufacturing still needs a product with extraordinary value per kilogram. Varda's answer is pharmaceuticals, especially drug crystallization in microgravity. The essay uses examples such as Keytruda research and ritonavir crystallization to argue that microgravity can create crystal properties that may improve dosing, bioavailability, or formulation.

The more surprising business insight is Varda's early dual-use wedge. The company needs to return capsules from orbit at hypersonic speeds anyway. That creates a near-term government customer: the hypersonics community needs realistic flight testing environments. Government reentry and test revenue can subsidize the space-manufacturing loop and reduce upfront costs for pharmaceutical customers.

The article's deeptech lesson is that ambitious technical companies still need revenue within venture-fund time horizons. Varda's defense wedge is presented as the way to avoid remaining a pre-revenue R&D story for too long. The company can sell a real scarce capability while it climbs toward the larger prize of manufacturing high-value products in orbit.

The final thesis is that Varda is part of a broader expansion of the economic bounds of humanity. The first space businesses were satellites and communications. Varda's bet is that the next phase is not just going to space, but building an industrial economy there that makes valuable goods for Earth before eventually making goods for space itself.

## Key Ideas

- Cheap launch turns space from a destination into an input for new business models.
- The first commercial space-manufactured products need very high value per kilogram.
- Pharmaceuticals are attractive because small physical quantities can carry enormous economic value.
- A non-human-rated reentry capsule can be simpler and cheaper than crewed reentry systems.
- Dual-use demand can finance a deeptech company before the primary commercial market fully matures.
- The best wedge may be the byproduct capability required by the long-term mission.
- Deeptech companies need to show revenue early enough to remain financeable through the venture cycle.

- The user's highlighted passages concentrate on business-model architecture more than on space romanticism: venture time horizons, early revenue, cheap reentry, dual-use customers, high value-per-weight products, pharma margins, and the Keytruda microgravity example.
- The strongest reusable pattern is "mission-required capability as early product." Varda needs reentry to sell space-manufactured drugs; hypersonic testing customers need realistic reentry data; the overlap becomes the first revenue line.
- The source pairs well with the broader [[knowledge/company-building/Frontier Cost Collapse]] model: once a core access cost falls, adjacent high-value products become newly practical.

## Claims Worth Tracking

- Space manufacturing unit economics are most plausible for products with extremely high value per kilogram.
- Microgravity crystallization can materially change drug properties in ways that might improve dosing or delivery.
- Government hypersonic testing demand is urgent enough to support meaningful early Varda revenue.
- The "revenue within three years" rule is a useful financing constraint for deeptech companies, not just a VC slogan.

## Caveats

- Not Boring Capital is an investor in Varda, so this is partly an investor thesis.
- The technical plausibility of microgravity pharmaceutical manufacturing does not automatically imply regulatory, reimbursement, or manufacturing scale success.
- Defense revenue can create focus and capital, but it can also pull a company away from its original commercial market if not managed carefully.
- The article was written before later Varda mission outcomes, so the page should be updated if newer operating evidence changes the thesis.
