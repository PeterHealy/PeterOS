## What This Source Is

A MyPrivacy.blog article explaining Iran's surveillance stack: SIAM in cellular infrastructure, the National Information Network, foreign technology support, protester identification workflows, and the global risk of authoritarian surveillance export.

## Source Summary

The article opens with a protester receiving a warning text after attending demonstrations. The central claim is that Iran can identify protest attendance from phone-location data, then combine that with facial recognition, social-media evidence, SIM suspension, bank freezes, interrogation, and intimidation.

The technical core is SIAM, the Subscriber Identity and Activity Monitoring platform. The article describes SIAM as a network-level government control panel embedded in cellular infrastructure. It can query internet history, call and SMS records, identity data, billing data, and location. It can also suspend service, force devices onto less secure networks, or redirect calls.

SIAM is paired with the National Information Network, Iran's domestically controlled internet architecture. The NIN can keep internal services operating while cutting or filtering international connectivity. This creates a hybrid model: enough connectivity for economic and government functions, enough isolation to prevent protest information from escaping easily.

The article attributes parts of Iran's surveillance capacity to Chinese and Russian technology transfer. It names Huawei, ZTE, Tiandy, Great Firewall methods, PROTEI, DPI systems, SMS infrastructure, signaling components, GPS disruption, and cyber cooperation as part of a broader authoritarian technology pipeline.

The protester-identification section lays out a step-by-step nightmare: location queries around protest sites, IMSI catchers, identity-tied SIM registration, facial recognition, social-media scraping, financial controls, and delayed enforcement. The point is that surveillance becomes the investigation rather than a supplement to it.

The larger warning is global. Iran is presented as both a user and testbed for tools that can spread to other governments. The risk is not only one country's repression but a modular surveillance model that other states can adopt.

## Key Ideas

- Modern repression can operate through ordinary telecom infrastructure rather than dramatic hacking.
- Identity-tied SIM registration makes phone location function like person location.
- Internet kill switches can be partial and surgical, preserving domestic control while blocking external visibility.
- Surveillance and financial controls become more powerful when they are integrated.
- Authoritarian states can share censorship, DPI, jamming, and cyber-surveillance techniques.
- Protest risk changes when attendance itself can be reconstructed from network logs.
