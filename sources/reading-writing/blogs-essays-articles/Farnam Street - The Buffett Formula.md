## What This Source Is

A Farnam Street article distilling Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger's learning practice: read heavily, think independently, apply ideas, and become a little smarter each day through compounding knowledge.

## Source Summary

The article presents Buffett and Munger as examples of continuous learning. Their success is framed less as raw genius and more as a long-running practice of reading, thinking, and turning facts into judgment.

Buffett's famous advice is to read hundreds of pages every week because knowledge compounds like interest. Todd Combs is used as an example of someone who took the practice seriously, tracked his reading, and turned it into career-changing insight.

The article emphasizes that reading is not the same as consuming opinions. Buffett describes his work as gathering facts and information, then seeing whether they lead to action. The thinking time matters as much as the intake.

Munger's "be your own most valuable client" advice reframes time allocation. An hour spent improving your own understanding can compound, while an hour lost to transient distraction usually disappears without durable return.

The final section warns that reading alone is insufficient. Knowledge has to be articulated, tested, and applied. If you cannot explain what you think, you probably do not yet know what you think.

## Key Ideas

- Knowledge compounds when reading is consistent and cumulative.
- Reading plus independent thinking is different from consuming other people's conclusions.
- An hour invested in your own learning can have compounding returns.
- The goal is not information hoarding; it is better judgment and action.
- Understanding improves when you explain an idea in your own words.
- Consistency matters more than appearing unusually smart.
