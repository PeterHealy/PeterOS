# Agent-First Startups

## Why This Matters

This thesis points at a possible shift in company-building: instead of shipping traditional dashboard-first SaaS, founders may be able to build agent-first products that sit on top of incumbent systems and deliver outcomes directly.

## Working Notes

- Greg Isenberg's April 18, 2026 tweet frames the opportunity as unusually large and time-sensitive.
- The central claim is not just "add AI to SaaS" but "treat incumbent SaaS as backend infrastructure."
- The fuller user-provided text turns this into a GTM thesis, not just a product thesis.
- The proposed stack is: incumbent SaaS as system of record, agent as worker, founder as workflow expert, outcome pricing as monetization, and distribution as moat.
- The implied wedge is an ugly workflow that already leaks across dashboards, spreadsheets, phone calls, and service teams.
- The biggest product question is whether the startup can own the workflow and the outcome while the incumbent owns storage, permissions, and reporting.

## Contradictions

- Buyers may still want dashboards, logs, overrides, and audit trails even if the agent does the default work.
- In many categories, the incumbent product is not just a database; it also embeds workflow, permissions, reporting, and trust.
- Agent-first surfaces may win attention before they win reliability or procurement trust.
- Outcome pricing only works cleanly where attribution is measurable and not heavily contested.
- Distribution may matter, but buyer trust, switching friction, and proprietary data can still be larger moats than audience alone.

## Leads To Follow

- Collect examples of startups building on top of Salesforce, HubSpot, or Workday as backends.
- Track where incumbents are actually becoming more API-first or headless.
- Compare pure agent-first products with hybrid agent-plus-dashboard products.
- Look for categories where users care more about outcomes than about direct interaction with the underlying software.
- Track categories where implementation-service cost is large relative to software license cost.
- Study whether ex-operators outperform model-first generalists in early vertical AI companies.
- Collect examples of companies pairing media or audience with workflow software as a distribution wedge.

## Open Questions

- Which niches are most exposed to the "agent as product, SaaS as backend" pattern?
- What minimum control surface is still required for trust?
- Does this thesis apply more to internal tooling, SMB workflows, or enterprise systems?
- What distribution strategy works best for agent-first products?
- Which workflows are measurable enough to support true outcome pricing?
- Where does "no implementation team" hold up versus break down?
